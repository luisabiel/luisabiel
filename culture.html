Culture of the City.

Hong Kong's culture is a unique blend of Chinese and Western influences.

A highly cosmopolitan city, with people from all over the world living and working together.

This diversity is reflected in Hong Kong's culture, which is rich in festivals, traditions, and food from around the world.

Click through the slides. They're Hong Kong's cultural staples.

If you ask people what dish comes to mind when you say "Hong Kong", the answer is probably Dim Sum over half the time.

Characterized by dumplings, savory buns, and flavorful small bites.

It's a symbol of communal dining and tradition.

Next up is what most tourists don't notice when they walk around the city.

Bustling sidewalks lined with street food.

With iconic choices such as Gai Daan Jai [egg waffles], Ngau Yuk Che [beef skewers], Cheung Fun [rice rolls], Gaa Lei Yu Dan [curry balls] & more.

Symbolizing joy and good fortune, the festivals of the city are always lit up by hundrends of Chinese Lanterns.

Intricate designs & bright colors are the first things you notice when you look at them.

Commonly seen during Hong Kong's Mid-Autumn Festival when families gather outside and share mooncakes.

A photo of a costume held up by multiple artists. The Lion Dance.

Combining artistry, acrobatics and cultural symbolism, the dance needs two performers to either be in the costume or hold it up. They make the lion move in a dynamic way while following a rythm.

A combination of cymbals, drums and gongs, the lion is believed to bring prosperity and protection from evil spirits. Typically performed during the Chinese New Year.

Towering skyscrapers fusing and coexisting with traditional Chinese buildings.

That's what makes up the city's Iconic Skyline. A testament to it's dynamic and cosmopolitan identity.

It's what I look to when asked about architecture.

This is the Chi Lin Nunnery, one of Hong Kong's many Temples. It shows how heavily believe in embracing and preserving tradition.

For tourists and residents alike, it's a glimpse into the the city's past buildings.

From the iconic Wong Tai Sin Temple to the historic Man Mo Temple, these sacred spaces provide a harmonious blend of tradition and modernity within the vibrant tapestry of Hong Kong's cultural landscape.
